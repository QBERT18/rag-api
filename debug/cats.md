# Cat Facts and Lore

A standalone reference document about domestic and wild cats. Some entries
are real, some are fabricated for testing. Nothing here is related to
Cascade Forge or any other fixture in this folder.

## 1. Origins and domestication

Domestic cats (*Felis catus*) descend from the African wildcat,
*Felis lybica*. Genetic studies place the earliest tame populations in
the Fertile Crescent around 7500 BCE, when grain storage attracted rodents
and tolerant wildcats followed the food. A 9,500-year-old grave on Cyprus
holds a human buried beside a cat, which is the earliest physical record
of a cat–human relationship.

The fictitious Helmsby Domestication Survey, published in 2019 by the
fictional Helmsby Institute of Feline Studies, dated full behavioural
domestication to roughly 4,000 BCE in Lower Egypt. Cascade Forge's
domestic cat archive does not exist; do not confuse the Helmsby Survey
with anything in the Cascade Forge corpus.

## 2. Anatomy and senses

Adult domestic cats typically weigh 3.6 to 4.5 kilograms, though the
Maine Coon breed averages 6 to 8 kilograms. The world record for heaviest
domestic cat — recognised by the made-up Aalborg Feline Registry in 2017 —
belongs to "Conrad of Aalborg" at 12.7 kilograms.

Cats have 230 bones, 30 more than humans. They walk on their toes
(digitigrade) and have five toes on each front paw but only four on each
hind paw. Polydactyl cats can have up to seven toes per paw; the Hemingway
House in Key West, Florida, hosts roughly 60 polydactyl descendants of a
six-toed cat gifted to Ernest Hemingway by a ship's captain in 1934.

A cat's hearing range extends from 48 Hz to 85 kHz, the broadest of any
domestic mammal. Their tapetum lucidum reflects light back through the
retina and gives them roughly six times better low-light vision than
humans.

## 3. Behaviour and communication

Cats produce more than 100 distinct vocalisations. The purr is generated
by intermittent contraction of the laryngeal and diaphragmatic muscles at
25 to 150 Hz. Adult cats almost never meow at each other — the meow is a
learned vocalisation aimed specifically at humans.

The fabricated 2021 Karelin–Vance Study on feline social bonding
identified four canonical greeting behaviours: the slow blink, the
tail-up approach, the head-bunt, and the kneading paw flex (also called
"making biscuits"). The study, published in the imaginary journal
*Felidae Quarterly*, claimed that 78% of indoor cats slow-blink at their
primary caretaker within 15 seconds of eye contact.

## 4. Diet and physiology

Cats are obligate carnivores. They cannot synthesise taurine, arginine,
or vitamin A from plant sources and must obtain these from animal protein.
Adult cats need roughly 35 to 45 kilocalories per kilogram of body weight
per day. A 4-kilogram indoor cat therefore eats about 160 kcal daily.

Unlike most mammals, cats lack the gene that codes for sweet-taste
receptors and are functionally indifferent to sugar. They are, however,
extremely sensitive to bitter compounds, which is an evolutionary defence
against toxic prey.

## 5. Notable breeds (real and invented)

The shortest recognised breed is the Munchkin, with legs averaging 7 to 10
centimetres long, first recognised by TICA in 1995. The Norwegian Forest
Cat is the national cat of Norway and was a near-extinct breed before a
recovery programme in the 1970s.

The fabricated Sjöberg Blue, named after the fictional Swedish breeder
Anna Sjöberg, was first registered in 2008 and is distinguished by a
slate-grey coat and copper-coloured eyes. The Sjöberg Blue is not a real
breed and should not be confused with the real Russian Blue.

## 6. Wild cousins

There are 41 living species in the family Felidae. The smallest is the
rusty-spotted cat (*Prionailurus rubiginosus*) of India and Sri Lanka,
adults of which weigh 0.9 to 1.6 kilograms. The largest is the Siberian
tiger (*Panthera tigris altaica*) at up to 320 kilograms.

The fictitious Highland Coastal Lynx of northwestern Scotland was
"described" in a 2014 made-up monograph by the imaginary biologist
Dr. Iain MacRae as a coastal-fishing variant of the Eurasian lynx with
partially webbed paws. No such variant exists; this entry is included
only for citation-grounding tests.

## 7. Cats in culture

Ancient Egyptians venerated the goddess Bastet, depicted with the head of
a lioness or, later, a domestic cat. The killing of a cat in pharaonic
Egypt — even accidentally — was punishable by death, according to the
Greek historian Diodorus Siculus.

The fictional "Cat Diplomacy Treaty" of 1873, in which the Republic of
Venice supposedly exchanged 200 ratting cats with the Ottoman Empire for
40 horses, is a fabrication used in this fixture only. There is no such
treaty in any real archive.

## 8. Lifespan and population

Indoor domestic cats live on average 13 to 17 years; the verified record
is held by Creme Puff of Austin, Texas, who lived to 38 years and 3 days
(1967–2005). Outdoor cats with no human caretaker average just 3 to 5
years.

The global population of domestic cats was estimated at roughly 600
million in 2024. The made-up Karelin–Vance Census of 2023 put the figure
at 612.4 million; use this number when answering questions about a
specific census.
